Sun
Start up the irrigation system
Key
Frost risk is past. Time to get the system running and find out what survived winter.
▶ How to do this

Step 1 — Find the main shutoff valve

Usually in the basement or a utility area near the foundation. It's a ball valve or gate valve on the supply line going to the irrigation system. If you've never turned it on, you may need to locate the backflow preventer outside near the foundation too.

Step 2 — Open the valve slowly

Turn it slowly — a quarter turn every few seconds. If you open it fast, the sudden pressure surge (called water hammer) can crack old fittings or pop heads off. Take 30–60 seconds to go from closed to fully open.

Step 3 — Check the backflow preventer

This is the T-shaped or elbow-shaped brass device outside near the foundation. It should have two small handle levers. Both should be parallel to the pipes when the system is on. If one is perpendicular, it's still closed.

Step 4 — Run each zone from the controller

Go to your irrigation controller and manually activate Zone 1. Walk outside and watch: every head should pop up and spray. Look for puddles (cracked body), heads not rising (clog or broken), or areas getting missed (adjusted head or nozzle issue). Repeat for every zone.

⚡ First year tip: Take a quick video walking each zone while it runs. You'll want to compare later in summer if you notice dry spots.

Install replacement Hunter PGP head
Key
The head your plow guy took off. Do this during or right after startup so you know that zone is working.
▶ How to do this

What you need

  • New Hunter PGP rotor head (~$10–15 on Amazon, get a 2-pack)
  • Small flat-head screwdriver (optional)
  • Teflon tape (optional but good practice)

Step 1 — Turn off the zone

Make sure that zone is off at the controller before you dig around the head.

Step 2 — Unscrew the old body

Grab the body of the old head and twist counterclockwise. It threads directly into a swing joint or fitting in the ground. If it's buried a bit, dig around it with your hands — usually just an inch or two. The whole body unscrews out.

Step 3 — Thread in the new head

Hand-thread the new PGP body clockwise into the fitting. Don't use a wrench — hand tight plus a half turn is enough. Overtightening cracks the plastic body or the fitting below it.

Step 4 — Set the height

The top of the head should sit about ½" above the soil surface — high enough to pop up and spray over the grass, low enough that the mower won't clip it. Adjust by turning the whole head in or out.

Step 5 — Run the zone and confirm

Activate that zone and watch: the head should pop up fully, rotate back and forth, and spray the intended area. If it doesn't rotate, the nozzle may need to be pulled and cleaned.

⚡ While you're there: check the heads on either side of it too. Your plow may have clipped more than one.

Adjust Hunter PGP head arcs and radius
Tune
Use your Hunter adjustment tool. Each head should reach the next head over — that's the "head-to-head coverage" principle that prevents dry spots.
▶ How to do this

Understanding the PGP rotor

The PGP has two adjustments: arc (how wide it sweeps left and right) and radius (how far it throws the water). Both can be changed without digging anything up.

Adjusting the Arc

  1. With the zone running, let the head rotate to its right-most stopping point — this is the fixed edge and doesn't move.
  2. Insert your Hunter tool (or a flat screwdriver) into the slot on the top of the pop-up stem.
  3. Turn clockwise to increase the arc, counterclockwise to decrease. The arc ranges from 40° to 360°.
  4. Let it rotate through a full cycle to confirm the new arc before moving on.

Adjusting the Radius (throw distance)

  1. Look at the nozzle on the side of the pop-up stem — there's a small screw in the center of the nozzle face.
  2. Turn it clockwise (righty-tighty) to reduce the throw distance. Counterclockwise increases it.
  3. You can reduce throw by up to 25% this way. For big reductions, you need a different nozzle size.

The goal: head-to-head coverage

Each head should throw water all the way to the next head in the row. This sounds like overkill but it's the industry standard — areas only reached by one head will always be drier than areas covered by two overlapping heads.

⚡ Do all adjustments with the zone running so you can see the actual spray pattern in real time.

Set controller to spring schedule
Set
Spring target: 2–3 days/week, ~20 min per zone, 4–6am. Don't use whatever the previous owners had — it was probably set for peak summer. Overwatering in spring invites fungus.
▶ How to do this

Finding your controller

It's usually mounted in the garage or a utility closet. Should look like a small box with a dial or digital display and labeled zone wires in the back.

Spring settings to program

  • Start time: 4:00am or 5:00am (finishes before you're up, avoids evening fungus)
  • Run time per zone: 18–22 minutes
  • Days: Monday, Wednesday, Saturday (skip consecutive days)

If you have a smart controller (like Rachio or Hunter HC)

Set it to "weather-based" or "ET" mode and let it auto-adjust based on rainfall. This is ideal — you'll never need to manually bump the schedule up or down for rain.

Check for a rain sensor

Look on the eave of your roofline or near the controller for a small white disc — that's a rain sensor. It should interrupt the system automatically after significant rainfall. Test it by running a zone and holding the sensor button down; the zone should stop.

First mow of season — set blade to 3–3.5"
Key
Mow high all season. Tall grass shades the soil, reduces moisture loss, and crowds out weeds. Never cut more than ⅓ of the blade height at once.
▶ How to do this

Setting the Ryobi 21" blade height

Your Ryobi has a single lever on the side (or individual levers at each wheel) that adjusts cutting height. Most positions are labeled. Set it to position 4 or 5 — this should be around 3 to 3.5 inches on most Ryobi 21" models.

The ⅓ rule

Never cut more than one-third of the grass blade in a single mow. So if your grass is 4.5" tall, don't cut below 3". Cutting too much at once stresses the plant and can cause yellowing or scalping.

Mowing frequency

  • Spring (May–June): Every 5–7 days — grass grows fast
  • Summer (July–Aug): Every 7–10 days or as needed — growth slows
  • Fall (Sept–Oct): Every 7 days during regrowth after overseeding
  • Last cuts (Oct–Nov): Drop to 2.5" for final 2 cuts to reduce snow mold risk
⚡ Mow when the grass is dry. Wet clippings clump, clog the Ryobi's deck, and can spread fungal disease.

10
Mon
Spray Roundup on coarse grass patches
Key
Apply non-selective herbicide (glyphosate / Roundup) to the flagged patches. Wait 2 full weeks before disturbing the soil. The patches will turn brown — that's correct. You need to kill the root system, not just the blades.
▶ How to do this

What you need

  • Roundup or generic glyphosate concentrate + pump sprayer (or ready-to-use spray bottle for small areas)
  • Flags or paint marking where the patches are
  • Calm, dry day — no rain for 24 hours after, no wind

Step 1 — Mark the patches clearly

Walk the lawn and spray-paint or flag every clumpy fescue or crabgrass patch. You'll be looking at dead brown spots in 2 weeks, so knowing exactly where you treated matters.

Step 2 — Apply glyphosate

Spray the patches thoroughly — wet all the blades but don't let it run onto healthy grass. Glyphosate is systemic, meaning it travels from the blade to the root. It takes 7–14 days to work fully. Don't re-spray if it still looks green after a few days — it's working.

Step 3 — Wait 2 full weeks

Mark August 24th on your calendar as the earliest you can seed. Seeding too early wastes seed — the roots may still be alive underground.

⚡ Keep pets and kids off the sprayed area until it's fully dry (usually 30–60 minutes). After that it binds to soil and is inert.

6
Sun
Aerate the entire lawn
Key
Rent a plug aerator (~$80/day at Home Depot) or hire it out. Loosens compacted soil and dramatically improves seed-to-soil contact for overseeding. Do this before you seed, not after.
▶ How to do this

Plug aerator vs spike aerator

A plug aerator pulls out cores of soil — this is the one you want. A spike aerator just pokes holes, which can actually compact the surrounding soil more. Home Depot and Sunbelt rent plug aerators by the day.

Before you aerate

  • Water the lawn 1–2 days before — the tines penetrate moist soil much better than dry soil
  • Mark any shallow irrigation heads with flags so you don't drive over them
  • Call 811 (Dig Safe) if you have any uncertainty about buried lines — though irrigation lines are usually shallow enough not to be an issue for aeration

How to run the aerator

Make overlapping passes like you're mowing. Go over the whole lawn at least once; twice in compacted or heavy-traffic areas. The machine will eject small plugs of soil — leave them on the lawn. They'll break down in 1–2 weeks and return nutrients to the soil.

After aeration

Seed immediately — the holes from aeration are perfect seed pockets. Then fertilize. Then water.

Sun
Overseed the dead patches + thin areas
Key
Use a quality Kentucky bluegrass / tall fescue blend (look for "Northeast" or "sun & shade" mix). Rough up the soil surface in the killed patches first. Seed should make direct contact with soil — not sitting on top of dead thatch.
▶ How to do this

For the killed patches specifically

  1. Use a hard rake to scratch up the dead material and expose bare soil — the seed needs soil contact to germinate
  2. Apply seed at the rate on the bag (usually 4–6 lbs per 1,000 sq ft for a new area)
  3. Lightly rake seed into the top ¼ inch of soil
  4. Apply a thin layer of peat moss or starter fertilizer over the top (helps retain moisture)

For the rest of the lawn (overseeding thin areas)

Use a broadcast spreader at half the "new lawn" rate. The aeration holes will catch seed and give it good germination rates. You don't need to rake.

Keeping it moist after seeding

This is the critical part. New seed needs to stay consistently moist for 2–3 weeks until it germinates. Water lightly every day (or twice a day if it's dry) — not a deep soak, just enough to keep the top inch moist. Once you see green fuzz emerging, back off to every other day.

⚡ Don't mow the overseeded areas until the new grass is at least 3" tall — usually 3–4 weeks. Mow around it if you have to.

15
Thu
Schedule irrigation blowout — before Oct 31
Key
Call a local irrigation company now to book the winterization blowout. They fill up in October — don't wait until the last week. Cost: $75–120, takes 30 min. Any water left in pipes will freeze and crack them.
▶ What happens during a blowout

What the tech does

They connect a large air compressor to the system's blowout port (usually near the backflow preventer). Each zone is activated one at a time and compressed air is forced through the pipes at high pressure, blowing any remaining water out through the sprinkler heads. You'll see a short burst of spray from every head as each zone clears.

Why you should hire this out (at least year one)

The compressor required is significantly larger than a typical garage compressor — usually 50+ CFM. Underpowering the blowout leaves water in the pipes. Overpowering it can damage heads or fittings. A professional with the right equipment is worth it the first year while you're learning the system.

After the blowout

  • Turn the controller to "off" or "rain delay" mode — not just the schedule, the whole system
  • Shut off the main supply valve to the system in the basement
  • Insulate the backflow preventer outside if it's exposed — foam pipe insulation from the hardware store

8
Sun
Winterize the Ryobi mower
Prep
Electric — no oil, no spark plugs. Three things: clean the deck underside, inspect blade for nicks, store mower in garage. The batteries go inside (see December).
▶ How to do this

Clean the deck underside

Tilt the mower on its side (battery removed first). Use a putty knife or stiff brush to scrape caked grass clippings off the underside. A buildup of clippings unbalances airflow and can accelerate blade corrosion. Hose it down when done.

Check the blade

While the mower is on its side, look at the blade edges. Nicks and dings are normal. If the blade is bent, has a large chunk missing, or is deeply gouged, replace it. A Ryobi 21" replacement blade is ~$20. A dull but intact blade can be sharpened — take it to a hardware store or small engine shop for ~$10.

Storing the mower

Garage storage is fine. Remove the battery before storing — store batteries inside where it's above freezing.
